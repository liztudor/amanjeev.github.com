Macho Developers and the Clique

I recently met someone during my travel to work, in the train. I will not name this certain someone but just know that he is apparently a talented FOSS contributor and is related to GNU.org. I will call him Mr. X. In general, X was friendly. He caught me writing some code in the train and assumed I was writing JavaScript.

X: Are you writing JavaScript?

Me: Yes.

X: Anything that colorful could only be JavaScript.

[ (ummmm... it's the editor]

Me: Are you a programmer?

X: I don’t write JavaScript. I am a C hacker. Kernel hacker.

[At this point I did not realize there was a bit mocking tone to his "I don't write JavaScript"]

Me: Wow!

After this, I didn’t have to ask a lot and a lot of talking was done by Mr. X. Mostly nice talk but a couple of things however, got stuck with me since that day.

The clique

He said, “He does not write anything that cannot be written with C“. There was a sense of superiority in his style of conversation. “Moi poor web developer”, I thought. I like open-source software and use it everyday. I really wish that this attitude changes amongst the developer community. I really respect your knowledge but it hurts if you lack humility and respect for others, especially the ones who aspire to contribute and support the open-source software and community. This attitude makes it harder for everyone. I am glad that there are posts like this which support this view.

Us Macho Programmers

macho : aggressively virile; a man who is aggressively proud of his masculinity.

I heard the phrase Us macho programmers at least thrice in the conversation. I do not appreciate this. What this conveys to me is that the real programmers are the knights, which in turn, are only masculine. This equates macho with programmers. I have nothing much to say on this issue except that it discouraged me from pursuing being a part of this group he was talking about which I mentioned about in the clique. If I, a male programmer, got discouraged then I can’t imagine what do female programmers feel or think with these kind of phrases being thrown around regularly by people who are leading or are part of the open-source movement. Programming is not a macho profession or something that men control. There maybe more men doing it but I believe that this is the profession’s loss. I am glad that some people correct themselves and I hope it happens more often. Another good read is this blog on programming as a male profession on Fog Creek’s blog by Anna Lewis.

In essence, I was excited in the beginning to be sitting and chatting and getting to know the insights of the world of core-members and GNU but it was rather disappointing because of the conversation.
